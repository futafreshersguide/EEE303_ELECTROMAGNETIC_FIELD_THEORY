Exploratory Notes & Essential Formulas

While Electrostatics deals with charges at rest, Magnetostatics is the study of magnetic fields generated by steady (DC) currents. A constant current produces a magnetic field that is constant in time. Remember: magnetic field lines never start or stop at a point—they always form continuous, unbroken loops!

1. Magnetic Field Intensity (H) vs. Magnetic Flux Density (B)

Just like we had $E$ and $D$ in electrostatics, we have two distinct magnetic field vectors. You must know the difference to pass.

  • Magnetic Field Intensity (H): This is the "magnetizing effort" driven purely by the free source currents. It is completely independent of the material medium it exists in. Its unit is Amperes/meter (A/m).
  • Magnetic Flux Density (B): This is the actual, physical magnetic field that results from $H$. It depends heavily on the material (the permeability, $\mu$) the field penetrates. Its unit is Tesla (T) or Webers/m².
  • The Relationship: B = μ H = μo μr H (where $\mu_o = 4\pi \times 10^{-7}$ H/m is the permeability of free space).
2. Biot-Savart Law (The Magnetic Coulomb's Law)

The Biot-Savart Law is the fundamental law for calculating the magnetic field intensity $H$ produced by a tiny, differential current element ($I dl$). It follows the Right-Hand Rule.

  • General Formula: dH = (I dl × aR) / (4π R2)
  • Infinite Straight Wire: If you have an infinitely long wire on the z-axis carrying current $I$, the magnetic field circulates around it in the $\phi$ direction. Notice the denominator is $2\pi\rho$, not $4\pi r^2$! H = I / (2πρ) aφ
3. Ampere's Circuit Law (The Magnetic Gauss's Law)

Ampere's Law is incredibly powerful when your current distribution has high symmetry (like a long wire or a coaxial cable).

  • Integral Form: The circulation (line integral) of $H$ around any closed path is exactly equal to the direct current enclosed by that path. ∮ H · dl = Ienc = ∫ J · dS
  • Differential Form: Applying Stokes' theorem proves that a steady current density ($J$) is the source of the magnetic field's "swirl" (curl). ∇ × H = J
4. Gauss's Law for Magnetism (No Monopoles!)

Because magnetic field lines form closed loops, whatever flux enters a volume MUST also exit it. There are no isolated magnetic charges (magnetic monopoles).

  • Integral Form: The net outward magnetic flux through any perfectly closed surface is exactly zero. ∮ B · dS = 0
  • Differential Form: The divergence of a magnetic field is zero everywhere. It is strictly a solenoidal field. ∇ · B = 0
5. Magnetic Forces & Lorentz Equation

A magnetic field is "blind" to stationary charges. It ONLY exerts a force on a charge if it is moving.

  • Magnetic Force (Fm): The force is a cross product, meaning it pushes perpendicular to both the velocity ($v$) and the magnetic field ($B$). Fm = Q(v × B)
  • Work Done is ZERO: Because the magnetic force is always perpendicular to the direction of motion, a static magnetic field does zero work on a moving charge. It can only change the particle's direction, never its speed or kinetic energy!
  • Lorentz Force Equation: If a particle moves through a region with BOTH an electric field and a magnetic field, the total force is: F = Fe + Fm = Q(E + v × B)
⚠ O'YEMI'S MAGNETOSTATIC BOUNDARY TRAPS:
Just like electrostatics, fields bend when crossing from one magnetic material to another (e.g., from air into iron).

1. Normal Components: The normal component of the Magnetic Flux Density ($B$) is ALWAYS continuous.
Bn1 = Bn2

2. Tangential Components: The tangential component of the Magnetic Field Intensity ($H$) is continuous ONLY if there is no surface current density ($K$) exactly on the boundary.
Ht1 - Ht2 = K. (If $K = 0$, then $H_{t1} = H_{t2}$).
