Exploratory Notes & Essential Formulas

Vector calculus is the heartbeat of electromagnetic theory. It explains how fields change over space. The secret to passing EEE 303 is memorizing the scale factors ($h_1, h_2, h_3$) for each coordinate system, because they completely change the formulas for Divergence and Curl.

1. The Gradient (∇V)

Operates on a scalar field ($V$) to produce a vector. It points in the direction of the maximum rate of increase of the function. It is heavily used to find the Electric Field from Electric Potential ($E = -\nabla V$).

  • Cartesian: ∇V = (∂V/∂x)ax + (∂V/∂y)ay + (∂V/∂z)az
  • Cylindrical: ∇V = (∂V/∂ρ)aρ + (1/ρ)(∂V/∂φ)aφ + (∂V/∂z)az
  • Spherical: ∇V = (∂V/∂r)ar + (1/r)(∂V/∂θ)aθ + (1/(r sinθ))(∂V/∂φ)aφ
2. The Divergence (∇ · A)

Operates on a vector field ($\mathbf{A}$) to produce a scalar. It measures the net outward flux of a field per unit volume from a point. A positive divergence means the point is a source; a negative divergence means it's a sink.

  • Cartesian: ∇ · A = ∂Ax/∂x + ∂Ay/∂y + ∂Az/∂z
  • Cylindrical: ∇ · A = (1/ρ) ∂(ρAρ)/∂ρ + (1/ρ) ∂Aφ/∂φ + ∂Az/∂z
  • Spherical: ∇ · A = (1/r2) ∂(r2Ar)/∂r + (1/(r sinθ)) ∂(Aθ sinθ)/∂θ + (1/(r sinθ)) ∂Aφ/∂φ
3. The Curl (∇ × A)

Operates on a vector field to produce another vector. It represents the macroscopic circulation or "swirl" of a field. Magnetic fields always have curl because they form closed loops.

In Cartesian, you use the standard determinant matrix. For Cylindrical and Spherical, beware of the scale factors!

  • Cylindrical (The Trick): You must multiply the $A_\phi$ component by $\rho$ inside the derivative, and divide the overall $\mathbf{a}_z$ term by $\rho$.
  • Spherical (The Trap): You must multiply $A_\theta$ by $r$, and $A_\phi$ by $r\sin\theta$ inside the determinant.
⚠ O'YEMI'S NULL IDENTITIES:
These will save you 10 minutes of calculus on the exam if you spot them:
1. The curl of a gradient is ALWAYS zero: ∇ × (∇V) = 0
2. The divergence of a curl is ALWAYS zero: ∇ · (∇ × A) = 0
If a question asks you to "Calculate the divergence of the curl of Vector F," just write 0 and move on!
4. The Great Theorems
  • Divergence Theorem (Gauss's Theorem): ∮S A · dS = ∫v (∇ · A) dv
    Converts a closed surface integral to a volume integral.
  • Stokes' Theorem: ∮L A · dl = ∫S (∇ × A) · dS
    Converts a closed line integral to an open surface integral.
